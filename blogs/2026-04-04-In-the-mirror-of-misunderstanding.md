# In the Mirror of Misunderstanding

The self-projection onto the misunderstood subject betrays only the self-understanding of the observer. He reveals the world he inhabits by imposing it upon the subject. His misunderstandings are mere self-betrayal, laying bare the driving force of his self-understanding. In refusing to let the Other count as Other, he avails himself of this insidious form of self-mirroring in place of thought.
 "The unnaive thought knows how little it reaches the thing thought, and must yet always speak as though it had it entire."¹ The naive thought, by contrast, proves itself only by subsuming all others beneath it. The Absolute can be nothing other than he, and he nothing other than the Absolute. That absoluteness, however, consists not in his accepting the limits of his knowledge and sublating of himself, but in his tyrannical dominion over the world around him. Not the understandings of man, but his misunderstandings, allow us to behold him in his almighty ugliness.


[^1]: T.W. Adorno, Negative Dialektik, Suhrkamp Verlag, 2003, S. 26
