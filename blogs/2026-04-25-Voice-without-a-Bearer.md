# Voice Without a Bearer

The refusal to allow the human of the lowest rank of society to produce his own art, and instead imposing upon him the art of those people who have never spent a single day in his world, is pure mockery. He is forced to adapt to this other world of art. He is not permitted to influence it — quite the contrary, this art must influence him, so that he might finally fit into the fine world's beautiful oil painting. His identity is no longer his own, but that of the artist who, in all his artistic brilliance, dares to shape it. The art that opposes the norm by feigning to the world a representation of the poor becomes precisely what it sought to avoid — philistine garbage. The revolting vanity of art about the poor, produced by the rich, is only commensurable with the ostentation of the sports car driver. This is not about justice, or the opportunity to represent an all too underrepresented group — no, this is about the elevation of the self. Pity is not the feeling required here, but egoism in its purest form. The rich man knows that through his feigned empathy he can raise his own place in the hierarchy of art; it is no gift, but merely a means of ascent. 
The supposedly cogent refutation of the objection that producing such art is presumptuous or paternalistic runs as follows: _"Is it not the task of art to place itself in hitherto unexplored circumstances, in order to make these accessible, even comprehensible, to everyone?"_ This refutation, however justified one may find it, has neither head nor tail. That art which purports to make such living conditions accessible to everyone does, in fact, something entirely different. It invents a living condition that does not exist — a kind of fabricated absolute — and proceeds to impose this upon its subject. Through this process, the properties of the subject are lost. They are eradicated. In their place stands only the perfect absolute, without flaw and entirely compliant to its creator. The subject may by now attempt to enter the exhibitions, but finds itself excluded; through high entrance fees and sheer snobbery it is locked out by the tyrants of art. Its own attempts are deemed not worth as much as those of the true artist. It is not that the subject is mute — rather, its voice is taken from it and appropriated by the artists, to be used against itself. 
To that extent the subject remains disempowered. The art that purportedly attempted to empower it desecrates it, and frames what little remains — for the sake of the philistines.
