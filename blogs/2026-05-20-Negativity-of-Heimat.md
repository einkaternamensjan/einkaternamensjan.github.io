# On the Negativity of Heimat

The following text is not finished. It will likely never be finished. I believe writing it to exhaustion would, however, defeat the purpose of the text; there should be no highest authority of interpretation. Every exposed passage of the text represents an intimation of its immanent becoming. Its weaknesses are starting points of new thinking, occasions for it. It was written with the conviction that philosophy is the most important thing there is, yet at the same time not so important.

*"Heimat, my sorrow, land in twilight's veil, Sky of blue above me, my joy, my all."*

The security of childhood, the serenity of the parental home — both fall prey to the mind's encounter with the world, inevitably torn apart by the hounds of harsh reality. The naive worldview forms within a person something they cannot do without for the rest of their lives: the Urheimat. It is a rigid, subjective conception of the world in a state of immaturity. It is doomed to fail; its fate does not lurk behind the curtains of a treacherous system, but is the very matter of reality itself. It is not possible for the Urheimat to endure. Its inescapable collapse is the starting point of adult life. The capacity to bear this death is the foundation of the capacity to imagine something new and better. The futile attempts to revive the decomposed corpse of the Urheimat lead a person only astray. The insight that it has finally perished brings one to the recognition of the true Heimat. The Urheimat is not a flower growing on the plant of Heimat; it is the subsequent fruit, which must simply die and rot so that Heimat can emerge from it. Some may try to put these flowers on display, as vain as if they need never wilt — but their blooming brilliance is as mortal as the creature that pursues their eternal preservation.

*"Gradually the sky grows darker* 
*And, lacerated, I raised this heart* 
*More tenderly. And I passed by* 
*Like snow in the fresh March air!"*

Heimat is the most important thing there can be for a person, yet it is at the same time not so important. Heimat does not arise from loving one's neighbour and lending a hand when they are in need. It does not arise through intention or striving at all. It simply arises — neither here nor there; here today, there tomorrow. Whoever has already imagined a Heimat, whoever has something in mind, has already had it slip away. _"You who will emerge from the flood / in which we have gone under / remember."_ Goodness is no longer goodness when an act is performed with the intention of goodness — it becomes perfidy, plain and simple. Heimat is no longer Heimat when it is brought about with precisely that intention — it becomes ideology, plain and simple. It has no form, no intellect, no reason. It is Heimat; everything to a person, yet at the same time nothing. It simply does not exist. **It does not exist at all.** No one has ever been a resident of humanity's Heimat, yet the direction of all hope is Heimat alone: _"A curse upon the false fatherland, / Where only shame and scandal flourish, / Where every flower is blighted early, / Where rot and mould delight the worm — / We weave, we weave."_ We weave Heimat, our Heimat, from the fibres of our true being. Although the land in which we live bestows on us the fruits of its labour, we do not need it. The land is dispensable, its existence irrelevant to our purposes. Heimat does not decay with the dissolution of borders or states.

*"The vine bears grapes,*
*The scapegoat bears horns."*

The flourishing botany of a field yields fruit and grain to people; rotting guilt yields death and shame. Who is to play judge of the world when the guilty have been exterminated?

"*Doctor: Now then, no excitement. When the Jew inspection is over, everything will be as before. No Andorran has anything to fear — we have that in black and white. I remain the local doctor and the innkeeper remains the innkeeper, Andorra remains Andorran…*
 *drumming*"

No overturning of powers permits this; the powers, as they are in and for themselves, must be dissolved. No power for anyone, all power for each and every one. Power excludes itself, cannot propagate further. When all possess the same power, no one possesses power. Furthermore, borders serve only to confuse us. The master-slave dialectic may have shown how consciousness observes itself by doing so through the eyes of the other. But what if we advance further. We renounce all borders, allow master and slave to melt into one another — where are we now? Souls transposed? The sovereign land is in and for itself, in and through the fact that it is for another in and for itself; that is, it exists only as something recognised. It cannot kill the other, cannot exterminate it, because in doing so it would exterminate itself as something recognised. The physical, tangible borders of a flesh-and-blood body do not serve here, however, to keep two bodies apart. The borders of a country, of a state, can overlap. They are permeable; the inhabitants can cross them without themselves perishing. With their abolition, both countries would indeed collapse — they would no longer exist as mutually recognising countries. The peoples, however, would continue to exist. Their existence as the human race thereby reveals itself to be independent of the formation and maintenance of any borders whatsoever. The need of a country to be recognised is superfluous to its people here. We are the people of the world; there is no other, there are no multiple peoples — there are only human beings and the earth. The manifold forms of a state are not that with which and through which a person identifies themselves and their Heimat.

*"…and not above, and not below, other peoples do we wish to be."*

How is culture to be preserved without enforced borders? The culture of a group, whether of a tribe or an entire people, requires no borders in order to propagate itself. What fixed borders separate the Bavarian from the Brandenburger? Does language not also function as the true bearer of this cultural copulation? Languages that can be learned offer permeable borders capable of preserving culture nonetheless. _"The limits of my language mean the limits of my world."_ In order to understand any given culture, one must inevitably acquire a kind of new language, which one can employ to apply one's knowledge to it, to interpret it, to make it one's own. Everything that presents itself to us as a border in this respect — this we wish to renounce. The enrichment of one's own world does not come about through the consumption and ingestion of materialistic objects; it cannot be brought about in this way. One succeeds in encountering it only through engagement with it, with our world, with nature: _"…and if an advantage is taken into account at all, it is at least not on our side. Not because we look down upon the child from the height of our strength and perfection, but because we look up from the limitation of our condition, which is inseparable from the vocation we have once attained, toward the boundless determinability in the child and its pure innocence — that is why we are moved, and our feeling in such a moment is too visibly mixed with a certain melancholy for its source to be mistaken."_ It is not about a bold mastery of the world through these thereby acquired capacities for understanding. What is at stake here is incorporating the world into ourselves, and ourselves into it. Everything unbounded, melting into itself.

*"Let him forbid what all desired,* 
*He's stirred a hornets' nest with that;*
*The realm they were supposed to guard* 
*Lies plundered now and desolate."*

A person does not feel at home in Heimat — that much is certain. A person who claims to have found their Heimat in a world that remains full of misery and suffering, exploitation and oppression, has found nothing. They have merely arranged themselves, come to terms with the pain of their fellow human beings. They are not in a state of Heimat — they are in a state of errancy. Their spirit contaminated by materialistic desires. Heimat is therefore only reachable by all or by no one. It is not to be found through the consumption of materialistic objects. The essence of Heimat does not suffer under blunt concepts and prejudices. _Constellations_ form the sky of its existence; constellations instead of blunt concepts. The elements of being that have been obliterated through the becoming of the Absolute or its idealisation — these we bring suddenly to appearance. As a bolt of lightning in the night illuminates everything around it, so do those constellations show us everything that was lost through the advent of the Absolute. Heimat is not a place; it occupies no location in the world. It is direction — but an indefinable one. Were it definable, it would long since have been dead: _"just don't jostle toward the angels."_ Heimat is resistance and submission, recovery and illness, improvement and deterioration. To précis the question directed at it is to deal it a deathblow. _"Here is a place for the gift of the poor from the abundance of his youth."_ The feeling of Heimat does not arise from what already is, but from what is not yet. Homesickness is in this way deceptive, for it bestows upon us idealised memories that are in fact images of what still _could_ be — a kind of _fore-memory_ (the concept beautifies the intuition, that which is intuited, and slightly blinds the observer here). _"…something which shines into the childhood of all and in which no one has yet been: Heimat."_ We see through these fore-memories that what does not exist can still become (it is assumed here that the word "must" may be used freely. Nothing _must_ necessarily come to pass. This delusion about the course of things only falls prey to misuse). Past and future in the same place — a synthesis of Heimat with Urheimat; but how is this even possible? From the past there remains as much for us to take as from the future. The present moment in which one finds oneself always contains the past and the future. The _Jetztzeit_ — the now-time — allows us to bring the dialectic of time in its becoming, of time altogether, to expression. The Urheimat grants us the frame that we require for the future Heimat. Its purpose, however, is not the framing of the subject, but its own disintegration. We need the frame so that we can imagine the world outside it — in order to see wherein eternal becoming consists.

*"I am sitting by the roadside.* 
*The driver changes the wheel.*
*I do not like the place I have come from.*
*I do not like the place I am going to.*
*Why do I watch the wheel-changing with impatience?"*

What Heimat is must not be spoken — it feeds on its own imponderable quality. It is absorbed and destroyed when one undertakes any measures to realise it; subsume Heimat or I'll give you a smack! What is needed instead of a Heimat that is hypostatised and reified is a Heimat that eludes its concept. In this respect it could not be hypostatised, reified — a Becoming-Heimat. Heimat not as a place that exists. Heimat as a _becoming_. An eternal project that never ceases to be in the process of becoming. Why should Heimat give precedence to anything? A Heimat that develops like a rhizome, keeps growing, without heeding the already existing structures of humanity. For why should it do that? It is, after all, not an acting being. The imponderable quality from which it feeds is therefore not imponderable quality plain and simple, but an imponderable quality that reveals itself when a person cannot manage to understand something. Heimat is therefore quite simply incomprehensible. Apart from the crude concept we have affixed to it, it is unfathomable, indeed senseless! Recognising this senselessness, this very absurdity of the fact, is the only capacity one requires in order to acknowledge Heimat for humanity — so as not to be forced to deny it to them out of sheer anxiety.

The attempt to salvage its bare parts and reconstruct it is enough to drive one mad. What is hoped for cannot be manufactured; abandoning hope, however, is equally impossible. It does not consist in living in excess or deficit, since this can only lead to its destruction; it resides above all not in the lofty dreams of an erring thinker whose thoughts are great but lack in content. The society of the product seeks to abolish becoming, holds it in horror. The _vitium exitiale_ of such a society consists in its inability to recognise the process of creation as a continuous becoming. A continuous fall perhaps — but continuous nonetheless. This society of the product attempts, however futile this may be, to administer this becoming from without, indeed to manipulate it. The process of growing does not, however, come from without. Like a growing plant that expands from within outward. It takes up the material that is made available to it from outside and produces, by means of inner biological mechanisms, the abundant foliage we observe. The fruits that will later form new plants are generated through that process. Their immanence dwells within them. Heimat, on the other hand, is not immanent — it _is_ immanence. It does not arise outside our world. Each person carries the seed of this immanence within them:

*"What were a God who only outward pressed,* 
*And set the All to circle on his finger's tip?* 
*It fits him best to move the world within his breast,*
*To cherish Nature in himself, himself in Nature's grip,*
*So that what in him lives and moves and has its being*
*Is never robbed of his strength, nor of his spirit's *freeing."*

It consists of optimism — the optimism of the self. The negativity, indeed pessimism of the matter, springs from its opposite: positivism. The capacity to express pure critique does not consist in permanently attempting to drag the world down, but quite the contrary: true optimism consists in being able to improve the world around one. The purpose of critique is pure positivity. The urge to see the world through rose-tinted spectacles is a consequence of inner darkening. Someone who must talk the world up to themselves admits in truth that they do not find it sufficient — but instead of any attempt at improvement, they deploy the talk of beautification, which is capable of deceiving no one but themselves. Within the soul that always manages to perceive the potential of a thing lies the source of all beauty, of change. The banality of a world that is, on the contrary, always beautiful enough is unbearable to the true optimist; they do not rest with the mere attempt to talk everything up, but go beyond it. This optimism of the self is what drives the wheels of movement forward. The optimism of the world, by contrast, serves only to console us when we believe ourselves unable to change our situation. _"The genuinely critical need of thought to awaken from the phantasmagoria of culture has been intercepted, channelled, fed to false consciousness."_ The need to liberate is tantamount to assigning it to the optimism of the self. _"We do not then confront the world in a doctrinaire fashion with a new principle, declaring: Here is the truth, kneel down before it! We develop new principles for the world out of the world's own principles."_ The hope for improvement seeks its home in critique, and there pursues its work.

Heimat cannot be conjured — it can only be conjured negatively, through negation. What it _is_ cannot be said. But what is it that cannot be put into words? _"The hovering thing can scarcely be brought properly into words…"_ — yet through its hovering before us, it _is_! _"A woman must become-woman, but in a becoming-woman of all men. A Jew must become-Jewish, but in a becoming-Jewish of the non-Jew. […] There is no subject of becoming except as a deterritorialised variable of the majority; there is no medium of becoming except as a deterritorialised variable of a minority."_ Heimat exists neither as subject nor object; it is to be thought beyond this disjunction. The negation of its being as subject or object allows it to elude all idealisation. In negating this difference, one gives its essence free rein; like a spectre, it is finally freed from the clanking chains of the subsumption of the past — a spectre is haunting Europe, the spectre of Heimat. Without this deterritorialisation, it is however not possible to negate this disjunction entirely. What would result would still be an identity that at best conforms to the disjunctive norm — _Quamquam non cogebar, tamen nolebam_. Without the dissolution of disjunctive thinking, of the dichotomy imposed upon identity, it is impossible to bring about essential change; _"wrong life cannot be lived rightly"_ — _coactus volui_. It cannot be distinguished a priori how Heimat presents itself in contrast to a false one. We have only the concept of it — no intuition, no tangible experience. How then is one to arrive at the recognition that it exists at all? By negating it. The negation of Heimat remains the only way out of this epistemological dead end. We can only recognise what it is _not_, and through this its outlines might be glimpsed. The lightning-swift strike of our method — _negative topology_ — does not show us the desired subject, but it locates it. We can observe the territory around it and thereby place the precise site of its essence. Concepts without intuitions are empty — yet the outlines of the empty negate the non-being of the content.

We do not wish to disinherit our children: I have been my father and my son. Heimat is not a place of disjunction. Either-or _is_ neither-nor; nothing is everything, but everything is much. One is not either man or woman, father/mother or son/daughter — but both and neither simultaneously. The pure concept obliterates the identifying. The identifying does not become identity — but the reverse. One loses the power of apprehension. That which does not fit into my image of _the foreign_ identity must be erased, smoothed over. _"Thinking that has gone astray with regard to identity capitulates easily before the unresolvable and fashions from the unresolvability of the object a taboo for the subject — which, whether irrationalist or scientific, should restrain itself from touching what is not like it, surrendering its weapons before the prevailing ideal of knowledge, thereby paying that ideal respect."_ Heimat is to take precedence over its concept; it is to dwell within the individual but not equally in the supra-individual such that it then synthesises the individual, through which alone that individual consequently identifies itself, thinks, and determines what it identifies with.

*"One fine day our masters will still order us*
*To conquer the ocean floor and the moon,*
*And it is already hard here, with this Russia,*
*And the enemy is strong and the way home unknown.*
*God preserve us,*
*And lead us home again,*
*Lead us home again…"*

The way home cannot be taught, and even less can it be learned. Any attempt to teach a person the "right" path, to show them where they are to go, will inevitably become the most insidious indoctrination. We cannot show our own what we see. The mountains that stand before us, the feeling of the great Other, point to the signpost. They do not, however, lead us further. What illuminates a person in the darkness of that hour belongs to them alone. Heimat is equally to be shared and not to be shared — for all and for no one. The cunning of Heimat: to elude everyone and at the same time to present itself. It channels itself through the veins of a person, flows like blood — yet is, a contrario, no elixir of life. It is not meant to let the light of heaven shine down on us.

*"The armies march,*
*The armies march,*
*Marie, do not weep.*
*When I return, I shall return under other banners,*
*Therefore do not weep."*

One exists in the suspension of one's becoming concept, regardless of whether one desires this or not. It is impossible to defend oneself against the immanent desecration. One's properties are not one's own — they belong to everyone but oneself. _De mortuis nil nisi malum._ Everyone one knows is long since dead. Becoming never tips over into being; the negation of non-being is its blossoming. Heimat lies in dying, yet it soars high above. It has not a single goal. It _becomes_.

*"Heaven shone, a blue one, peace came in to stay, Heimat, my sorrow, you, my brightness of day."*

Change presupposes that something remains by which the change can be seen.

*"Barblin: Here are his shoes. Don't touch them! When he comes back, these are his shoes."*
